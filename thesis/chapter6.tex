\chapter{Results and Discussion} \label{ch6_evaluation}
\markboth{Results and Discussion}{Results and Discussion}

Following the design and implementation of the modular blockchain system in Chapter \ref{ch4_implementation} and the educational curriculum in Chapter \ref{ch5_curriculum}, this chapter presents the evaluation of the proposed solution. The core objective of this thesis was to bridge the gap between theoretical concepts and practical engineering in distributed systems education. Therefore, the evaluation strategy must assess both the technical robustness of the software artifact and the feasibility of the educational material.

The evaluation is conducted in two distinct phases:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Phase I: Technical System Evaluation.} This phase involves quantitative benchmarking of the system. By subjecting the blockchain node to high-load stress tests (as defined in Module 3 \ref{ch5_module3}), we analyze critical metrics such as Transaction Throughput (TPS), Block Propagation Latency, and Resource Utilization. The objective of this phase is to verify that the educational blockchain system faithfully reproduces the practical constraints and performance trade-offs observed in real-world blockchain platforms, particularly those associated with the Scalability Trilemma, as exemplified by production systems such as Bitcoin and Ethereum \cite{buterintrilemma}.
    
    \item \textbf{Phase II: Curriculum Validation.} This phase involves a qualitative evaluation and assessment of the instructional effectiveness of the educational curriculum in Chapter \ref{ch5_curriculum}. An initial study was conducted with a group of master's students with varying backgrounds in Computer Science and Data Science. This phase focuses on evaluating the clarity of the instructional materials, the success rate of task completion, and the overall usability of the provided codebase.
\end{enumerate}

This dual-approach ensures that the system is not only technically sound but also accessible for its intended educational purpose.

\section{Phase I: Technical System Evaluation}
This section presents the quantitative evaluation of the implemented blockchain system. The experiments were designed to measure the performance characteristics of the two consensus mechanisms: Proof of Work (PoW) and Proof of Stake (PoS). The primary metrics considered were Transaction Throughput (TPS), Block Propagation Latency, and System Resource Utilization (CPU and Memory).

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
The benchmarking experiments were conducted on a MacBook Air (M3, 2024) equipped with an 8-core CPU and 16GB of Unified Memory running macOS. The blockchain network was simulated using Docker Desktop to containerize four distinct nodes (Node 1 to Node 4), emulating a distributed environment over a virtual bridge network. The workload was generated using the \textit{Benchmark Suite} developed in Module 3. The suite submitted a total batch of transactions to the network at a controlled submission rate to saturate the transaction pool. The metrics were captured by parsing the structured logs emitted by the nodes during operation.

\subsection{Throughput Analysis}
Transaction Throughput (TPS) is defined as the number of valid transactions committed to the blockchain per second. Table \ref{tab:tps_comparison} summarizes the results obtained from the stress tests.

        \hline

        \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Throughput Comparison: PoW vs. PoS}
    \label{tab:tps_comparison}

The results indicate a significant performance disparity. The Proof of Stake implementation achieved an average throughput of \textbf{16.03 TPS}, whereas the Proof of Work implementation was limited to \textbf{5.82 TPS}. This approximately 2.75x increase in throughput for PoS can be attributed to the elimination of the computationally intensive mining process. In PoW, the block generation time is artificially constrained by the difficulty target, whereas in PoS, the slot time is deterministic, allowing for more consistent and rapid block creation.

\section{Phase II: Curriculum Validation} \label{sec:phase2_validation}
While Phase I validated the technical correctness of the system, Phase II evaluates the pedagogical effectiveness of the curriculum. An initial study was conducted to assess whether students could successfully navigate the transition from theoretical knowledge to practical implementation using the provided learning modules.

\subsection{Initial Study Methodology}
The initial study involved a small group of five Master's level students, to represent the target audience of the course. The group consisted of three students specializing in Computer Science (CS) and two students specializing in Data Science and AI (DS-AI).

\subsection{Participant Feedback }
Qualitative feedback was collected to understand the root causes of the bottlenecks identified in Table \ref{tab:completion_rates}. The responses highlight specific educational hurdles related to distributed systems engineering.

\paragraph{Module 1: Network Fundamentals}
Despite the complexity of P2P networking, the scaffolding provided in the starter code proved effective. Participant P5 noted: \textit{"I was worried about the networking part, but the Starter code for the socket connector was very clear. I got my two nodes talking to each other on the first try using the Docker instructions."} This suggests that abstracting the low-level socket management allowed students to focus on the high-level logic of node discovery.

\paragraph{Module 2: Consensus Mechanisms}
This module presented the most significant engineering challenges, particularly regarding language-specific constraints. The CPU-intensive nature of Proof of Work exposed the limitations of Python's concurrency model.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The GIL Bottleneck:} Participant P2 reported: \textit{"I spent three hours debugging why my node kept disconnecting peers. I didn't realize that the `while True` mining loop was holding the Global Interpreter Lock (GIL), effectively pausing the heartbeat listener. I had to insert manual `sleep(0)` calls to yield execution."}
    \item \textbf{Algorithmic Complexity:} Participant P4 struggled with the dynamic difficulty adjustment: \textit{"The math for the dynamic difficulty adjustment was hard... My chain kept freezing because the difficulty got too high too quickly."}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Module 3: Performance Analysis}
While students successfully measured throughput, measuring latency proved conceptually difficult. Participant P3 remarked: \textit{"The throughput calculation was easy, but I struggled with measuring Latency."}

\paragraph{Module 5: System Extensibility}
The introduction of off-chain storage (IPFS) represented a steep learning curve. Participant P1 noted: \textit{"I couldn't finish the IPFS integration... It felt like a jump in complexity compared to the previous modules."} Conversely, the block-size limit task provided a clear lesson in protocol enforcement, with P5 noting: \textit{"It was challenging to understand how to measure a transaction size to keep the new generated blocks under the maximum block size."}

\section{Discussion} \label{sec:discussion}
This section interprets the results obtained from the evaluation of the two primary contributions of this thesis: the modular blockchain system implemented in Chapter \ref{ch4_implementation} and the educational laboratory curriculum designed in Chapter \ref{ch5_curriculum}. By synthesizing the quantitative performance metrics from Phase I and the qualitative participant feedback from Phase II, we assess the extent to which the research objectives outlined in Section \ref{ch1_introduction} have been met.

\subsection{Evaluation of the Blockchain System (Phase I)}
The quantitative data from the technical evaluation confirms that the implemented software artifact successfully models the fundamental behaviors of a distributed ledger, although with distinct performance characteristics inherent to its educational design.

\textbf{Architectural Validity:} The primary success of the system lies in its modular consensus engine. The ability to switch between Proof of Work (PoW) and Proof of Stake (PoS) without altering the underlying transaction pool or networking logic validates the software architecture. This modularity allowed for a direct, controlled comparison where the consensus algorithm was the sole independent variable. The results clearly demonstrated the theoretical scalability advantage of PoS (16.03 TPS) over PoW (5.82 TPS), providing students with empirical evidence of the Scalability Trilemma.

\textbf{Performance Constraints:} However, from an engineering perspective, the absolute performance of the system reveals significant limitations compared to production-grade networks. An optimized Ethereum node, for instance, can process hundreds of transactions per second under layer-2 scaling solutions. The observed 16 TPS ceiling in the PoS implementation is largely attributable to the runtime overhead of the Python interpreter and the Global Interpreter Lock (GIL), which serializes thread execution. While the system effectively illustrates the \textit{relative} difference between consensus mechanisms, its \textit{absolute} throughput renders it unsuitable for high-frequency trading or commercial deployment. Furthermore, the high variability in PoW block propagation latency inherently reflects the nature of the mining process; however, it also suggests that the p2p gossip protocol implementation lacks the advanced optimizations, such as compact block propagation, found in production-grade blockchain systems.

\subsection{Evaluation of the Educational Curriculum (Phase II)}
The study results indicate that the curriculum successfully achieves a balance between accessibility and technical depth, though specific pedagogical bottlenecks were identified.

\textbf{Educational Efficacy:} The high completion rates in Module 1 (Network Fundamentals) and Module 4 (Smart Assets) suggest that the "scaffolding" approach—where students build upon pre-existing code—is effective for introducing complex concepts. The "Glass-Box" design philosophy was validated by participant feedback; students reported a higher confidence in understanding consensus rules after being forced to implement the mining loop in Module 2. Which helps in supporting our goal in effectively bridging the gap between theory and implementation.

\textbf{Curriculum Friction Points:} Conversely, the drop in completion rates for Module 2 and Module 5 highlights a steep difficulty curve. The technical complexity of handling concurrency in Python (Module 2) became a distraction from the core blockchain concepts for some participants. While struggling with concurrency is a valuable systems engineering lesson, it risks frustrating students who lack a strong background in operating systems. Similarly, the conceptual leap required for the Hybrid Architecture in Module 5 suggests that the curriculum requires an intermediate module focused on cryptographic hashing and storage before introducing off-chain integration.

\section{Limitations} \label{sec:limitations}
While the results support the validity of the proposed framework, several limitations inherent to the study design and technical implementation must be acknowledged.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Constraints of the Study:} The curriculum validation was conducted with a group of only five participants. While this sample size is sufficient for qualitative usability testing and identifying major design flaws, it lacks the statistical number power to generalize the findings to the broader population of engineering students. Future iterations of this research would require a longitudinal study across multiple semester groups to effectively measure learning outcomes.
    
    \item \textbf{Runtime Environment Constraints:} The decision to implement the system in Python was driven by readability and educational accessibility. However, this choice introduces artificial performance bottlenecks that do not exist in compiled languages such as Rust, Go, or C++. Specifically, the Python GIL prevents true parallelism in multi-core systems, meaning the "Nodes" in the simulation competed for the same CPU cycles even when threaded. This limits the fidelity of the simulation regarding true parallel processing and race conditions.
    
    \item \textbf{Network Topology Simulation:} The experiments were conducted within a Docker bridge network on a single physical machine. This setup exhibits near-zero latency and perfect packet delivery, which drastically simplifies the challenges of a real-world production environment, where nodes face variable latency, packet loss, and partition attacks. Consequently, the Peer Discovery and broadcasting logic validated in this thesis may prove fragile if deployed over the public Internet without further testing.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary} \label{ch6_summary}
This chapter presented an evaluation of the educational blockchain framework. Phase I demonstrated that the system is technically robust, capable of reproducing key distributed systems phenomena such as the performance trade-offs between consensus algorithms. Phase II validated the curriculum's educational value, showing high completion rates for foundational tasks while providing significant engineering challenges for advanced students. The feedback collected highlights the effectiveness of the constructionist approach while identifying specific areas—namely concurrency management and off-chain integration—where the curriculum can be further refined to support student learning.
