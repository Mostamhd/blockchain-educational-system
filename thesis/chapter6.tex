This chapter presents the evaluation of the educational blockchain system and the accompanying curriculum designed in Chapter \ref{ch5_curriculum}. The evaluation is conducted in two distinct phases: a quantitative technical assessment of the system's performance under load, and a qualitative validation of the curriculum's feasibility through preliminary pilot testing with peer participants.

\section{Phase I: Technical System Evaluation} \label{ch6_technical_results}
To validate the educational value of the system, it is necessary to confirm that the prototype accurately mimics the behavioral characteristics of real-world distributed ledgers, specifically regarding the "Scalability Trilemma."

\subsection{Participant Feedback}
Qualitative feedback was collected regarding the clarity and difficulty of the tasks.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Clarity:} Participants reported that the "Hard Fork" task (Module 1) provided a clear visualization of how chain identity is formed.
    \item \textbf{Difficulty:} Some participants noted that Module 2 (Consensus) required a deeper understanding of Python's object-oriented inheritance than expected.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion} \label{ch6_discussion}
The results indicate that the developed system successfully bridges the gap between theory and practice. Technically, the system produces observable metrics that align with the theoretical bounds of distributed systems. Educationally, the "Constructionist" approach allowed participants to actively debug and verify these concepts.

\section{Limitations} \label{ch6_limitations}
It is important to acknowledge the limitations of this study. The curriculum was validated via a small peer group rather than a formal longitudinal study with students in a classroom setting. Additionally, the blockchain prototype simplifies certain cryptographic primitives (e.g., omitting complex UTXO scripting) to maintain educational accessibility.

\section{Summary}
This chapter demonstrated the technical robustness of the system and provided preliminary evidence of the curriculum's utility. The data confirms that the exercises effectively demonstrate key blockchain concepts such as consensus bottlenecks and network forking.
